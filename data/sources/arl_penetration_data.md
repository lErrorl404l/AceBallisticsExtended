## 1. ARL-TR-4632 — Ballistic Testing of SSAB Ultra-High-Hardness Steel for Armor Applications | Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Report No.** | ARL-TR-4632 |
| **ADA No.** | ADA493654 |
| **Title** | Ballistic Testing of SSAB Ultra-High-Hardness Steel for Armor Applications |
| **Authors** | Dwight D. Showalter, William A. Gooch, Matthew S. Burkins, R. Stockman Koch |
| **Date** | October 2008 |
| **DTIC URL** | https://apps.dtic.mil/sti/pdfs/ADA493654.pdf |
| **Distribution** | Approved for public release; distribution unlimited |

### Relevant Data

- Tests **V50 ballistic limits** of ARMOX 600T (570-640 BHN) and ARMOX ADVANCE (RC58-63, >650 BHN) ultra-high-hardness steels
- Plate thicknesses from **0.189 in (4.8 mm) to 0.472 in (12 mm)**
- Test methodology per **MIL-STD-662F** (up-and-down firing method for V50)
- Performance compared to **MIL-DTL-46100E** (standard HHA specification)
- Data used to generate new US military specification for UHH steel with two hardness classes: Class 1 (513-640 BHN) and Class 2 (>640 BHN)
- **Projectiles used**: Armor-piercing (AP) bullets — details in report tables
- Expanded on prior work by Rapacki et al.

| Field | Value |

| **Date** | May 2010 |
| **DTIC URL** | https://apps.dtic.mil/sti/pdfs/ADA530895.pdf |

### Relevant Data

- NOT a penetration test report — this is **aerodynamic/flight dynamic characterization**
- M855 ball ammunition tested in ARL Aerodynamic Experimental Facility (AEF) at simulated ranges out to **600 m**
- Aerodynamic coefficients, drag, yaw limit cycles, Magnus moment instabilities characterized

- Free-flight aerodynamic range tests on NATO 7.62mm family: **M59 ball, M80 ball, M61 AP, M62 tracer**

## 7. ARL-TR-4996 — Evaluation of Titanium-5Al-5Mo-5V-3Cr (Ti-5553) Alloy against Fragment and Armor-Piercing Projectiles

| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Report No.** | ARL-TR-4996 |
| **ADA No.** | ADA516831 |
| **Title** | Evaluation of Titanium-5Al-5Mo-5V-3Cr (Ti-5553) Alloy against Fragment and Armor-Piercing Projectiles |
| **Author** | Shane D. Bartus |
| **Date** | (2009-2010) |
| **DTIC URL** | https://apps.dtic.mil/sti/tr/pdf/ADA516831.pdf |
| **Distribution** | Approved for public release; distribution unlimited |

### Relevant Data

- V50 limits for ~13.9-mm Ti-5553 plates (STA and BASCA heat treatments)
- **Threats**: 0.30 cal AP M2 and 0.50 cal FSP
- Benchmarking against MIL-DTL-46077F and MIL-A-46077D for Ti-6Al-4V
- **Explicit V50 results**:

- Documents failure modes: shear plugging, delamination, disking, adiabatic shear bands
- Describes AP M2 core/jacket separation during penetration process

## 12. M855A1 EPR Published Test Data

### Source 1: DTIC ADA549416 — M855A1 Enhanced Performance Round (EPR) Media Day

| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **ADA No.** | ADA549416 |
| **Title** | M855A1 Enhanced Performance Round (EPR) Media Day |
| **Author** | Jeffrey K. Woods |
| **DTIC URL** | https://apps.dtic.mil/sti/tr/pdf/ADA549416.pdf |
| **Distribution** | Approved for public release |

**Key penetration data**:
- ARL live fire tests: M855A1 surpassed **7.62mm M80 ball** against soft targets on average
- **3/8" mild steel** penetration at ~400 meters (V50 range)
- Tested against 24 layers of Kevlar out to 1,000 m — Kevlar showed no sign of stopping it

| Field | Value |
|---|---|

- Exposed hardened steel penetrator nearly **twice as heavy** as M855's concealed penetrator

## 13. Published 7.62mm M61 AP Penetration Data

### Source 1: Stewart & Netherton (2019) — Defence Technology

| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Citation** | Stewart, M.G. and Netherton, M.D. "Statistical variability and fragility assessment of ballistic perforation of steel plates for 7.62mm AP ammunition." *Defence Technology*, 2019. |
| **DOI** | https://doi.org/10.1016/j.dt.2019.10.013 |

**Key penetration data**:
- Field tests: **70 rounds** of 7.62×51mm M61 AP fired at mild steel targets (Grade 350)
- Plate thicknesses: **10 mm to 32 mm**
- Measured: penetration depth, probability of perforation, muzzle/impact velocities, bullet mass, plate yield strength/hardness
- **Penetration depth variability**: approximately ±12%
- **50% perforation** of mild steel plate at **19-20 mm** thickness
- HHA armour (477-534 BHN per MIL-A-46100E) requires only **9.0 mm** for 5% perforation probability at 800 m/s
- Monte Carlo fragility curves developed for Grade 250, Grade 350, and HHA steels
- Plate thickness for 95% reliability (5% perforation risk) needs to be **11-15% thicker** than V50 thickness for mild steel; **20-30% thicker** for zero perforation

### Source 2: Cranfield University — Bokhari, Teagle, Horsfall (2016)

| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Citation** | Bokhari D, Teagle M, Horsfall I. "Terminal ballistics of 7.62mm armour piercing projectiles against spaced, oblique RHA plates." 29th International Symposium on Ballistics, May 2016. |
| **URL** | https://dspace.lib.cranfield.ac.uk/handle/1826/11910 |

**Key penetration data**:
- DAG 7.62×51mm AP (similar to M61/P80) against RHA plates
- Pseudo-monolithic reference: **3 × 5.6mm RHA plates** (16.8mm total) just stopped projectile at **850 m/s**
- Spaced plate configurations: 2.7mm front + 5.6mm rear RHA plates
- **Best mass efficiency (2.00)**: 2.7mm front at 10° obliquity, 400mm spacing
- Reducing spacing to 50mm only reduced efficiency by 17% (to 1.66)
- Optimal front-to-rear plate thickness ratio: **0.48:1**
- Key tradeoff: spacing and obliquity can be traded against one another

### Source 3: ARL-TR-4996 (Bartus) — see entry #7 above

V50 of 13.9mm Ti-5553 vs 0.30 cal AP M2: **729 m/s** (STA) and **697.7 m/s** (BASCA). The AP M2 is dimensionally and ballistically very similar to M61 AP (both use same steel core projectile in 7.62mm).

### Source 4: Catović (2024) — Numerical simulation study

| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Citation** | Ćatović, A. "Estimation of terminal ballistics parameters for several 7.62×51mm projectiles using numerical simulations." 2024. |
| **DOI** | https://doi.org/10.1177/15485129241240768 |

**Key data**:
- Numerical (Ansys Autodyn) comparison of M80, M61, P80/1, M80A1, M1158, M993 at **800 m/s** into Armox 500T
- M1158 had deepest penetration; penetrator density, hardness, and length are dominant factors
- Absence of gilding metal jacket in ogive section increased penetration capability
- Validation against available experimental data
